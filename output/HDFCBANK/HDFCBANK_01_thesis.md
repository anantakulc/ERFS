## 1.1 ONE-SENTENCE THESIS

HDFC Bank is a structurally dominant Indian private sector franchise — the country's largest private bank by assets — that has been materially de-rated (-62% from peak) on a combination of post-merger integration drag, NIM compression, a loan growth miss versus system, and an acute governance overhang from the unexplained chairman resignation; the bull case rests on the view that these headwinds are cyclical and quantifiable, the franchise moat is intact, the balance sheet is now positionally strong (LDR 95%), and at 1.4x FY28E P/BV the stock discounts scenarios that management's own operating metrics do not validate.

---

## 1.2 BACKGROUND & FRANCHISE CONTEXT

HDFC Bank (NSE: HDFCBANK; BSE: 500180) is India's largest private sector bank and the second-largest bank by total assets after SBI. As of March 31, 2026 (FY2026A):

- **Total Assets:** Rs43.6 trillion [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **Net Loans:** Rs29.4 trillion [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **Total Deposits:** Rs31.1 trillion [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **Market Capitalisation:** ~Rs11.5 trillion (~US$138bn at USD/INR ~83.3)
- **Branch network:** 9,700+ branches; 100 million customers
- **Technology:** ~$1bn cumulative tech spend over 5-6 years; mobile app: 60mn+ users

The bank completed its merger with HDFC Limited (the mortgage parent) effective July 1, 2023 — one of the largest corporate mergers in Indian history. This event created a balance sheet step-change (total assets roughly doubled), but also imported a Rs6.6 trillion borrowing book at elevated cost (FY24A), which has been the primary source of NIM dilution and the LDR overhang that defined FY24-FY26.

## 1.3 THE FIVE PILLARS OF THE BULL THESIS

### Pillar 1: LDR Constraint Is Now Resolved

The most cited structural bear concern — that the post-merger LDR of ~110% would cap loan growth and force sub-system expansion — has been empirically resolved:

- **LDR trajectory:** ~110% (December 2023) → 99% (3QFY26) → **95%** (4QFY26, March 2026)
- The 420bps QoQ decline in 4QFY26 was described by CLSA as "very impressive"
- Management confirmed on the April 18, 2026 earnings call: *"The loan deposit ratio is not a constraint. The regulator has come out and talked about it."*
- Period-end deposits grew Rs2.45 trillion in 4QFY26 alone — the largest single-quarter accretion in the bank's history
- Retail deposits as % of total accretion improved: sub-Rs3 crore deposits were 31% of FY25 net accretion, rising to 47% in FY26 — the granularity of the franchise is improving

**Implication:** The primary structural constraint on loan acceleration has been removed. FY27E loan growth of 13% (consensus) is achievable without deposit stress.

### Pillar 2: Franchise Moat — Unmatched at Scale

HDFC Bank's competitive position across key business verticals, as disclosed on the Q4FY26 call by CEO Sashidhar Jagdishan:

| Segment | Market Position |
|---------|----------------|
| Cash management | Market leader |
| Capital market settlements | 35-40% share |
| Bankers to issue (escrow) | 40-50% share |
| Country exports (trade finance) | 18-20% share |
| Card acquiring | 35-36% share |
| Credit card issuance | 21-22% of system |
| Card spends | 26-28% of system |
| MSME lending | #1 nationally; #1 in 15/28 states; top-2 in 25/28 states |
| Mortgage disbursements | Top-2 nationally |
| Auto loans | Market leader |
| Business Banking growth | ~20% YoY (FY26) |

This is a franchise built over 30 years. It cannot be replicated quickly. The bank acquires 6-8 million new customers per year; 22% of its 100mn customer base is under 30 years old — the future growth engine is already captive.

### Pillar 3: Asset Quality Is Best-in-Class and Improving

Asset quality metrics through 4QFY26 (March 31, 2026):

- **GNPA:** 1.15% (4QFY26) vs 1.30% (4QFY25) — improving YoY for multiple consecutive quarters [Company, Q4FY26 presentation]
- **GNPA ex-agriculture:** 0.91% — exceptionally low
- **NNPA:** 0.38% [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **Slippage ratio:** 0.90% (4QFY26) — declined 31bps YoY and 36bps QoQ; gross slippages in absolute terms declined YoY for three consecutive quarters (CLSA)
- **Credit cost:** 0.35% (4QFY26 actual); guidance 0.45-0.50% for FY26 (Citi)
- **Provisioning buffer:** 125bps of loans in contingent/floating reserves — significant shock absorber
- **PCR:** 67.2% [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- Mortgage book NPAs: "lowest in the industry on a book of our size" (DMD Kaizad Bharucha, Q4FY26 call)

The credit quality improvement through the rate-cutting cycle (125bps since Feb 2025) is a key differentiator — the bank maintained sub-1.2% GNPA despite industry-wide stress in unsecured retail.

### Pillar 4: ROA Stability Through the NIM Trough

The market narrative has focused on NIM compression as an earnings destroyer. The data tells a more nuanced story:

- **FY26A ROA:** 1.80% (Visible Alpha consensus) / 1.94% (management's reported figure based on total assets including IEA adjustment) [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **4QFY26 ROA:** 1.96% — the highest in 6 quarters
- ROA has been *stable* despite 125bps of rate cuts and 100bps of NIM compression since the merger — this is because cost discipline and credit quality offset the top-line drag
- Core C/I ratio fell from 40.5% (FY25) to 39.5% (FY26) — management confirmed opex growth running at 6.5-7%, below revenue growth
- Operating leverage is emerging: cost-to-assets at ~1.9% is "best-in-class" per CFO Vaidyanathan (Q4FY26 call)

**The ROA trajectory (consensus):** 1.80% (FY26A) → 1.79% (FY27E) → 1.86% (FY28E) → 1.95% (FY30E). The trajectory turns upward with balance sheet normalisation and margin stabilisation.

### Pillar 5: Balance Sheet Positioning for the Rate Normalisation Cycle

The India rate cycle paused in April 2026 due to India-Pakistan geopolitical tensions. However, several structural NIM tailwinds remain:

- **Borrowings:** Declined to 11% of balance sheet (FY26) from 21% (FY23) — remaining ~Rs4.9 trillion of borrowings at elevated cost will continue to be refinanced at lower rates as they mature [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- **Time deposit repricing:** CFO confirmed ~40-50bps of the 125bps policy rate cut has transmitted into deposit costs so far; further repricing of existing time deposits (5-6 quarter repricing cycle) will provide continued CoF relief
- **NIM 4QFY26:** 3.38% — already +3bps QoQ vs 3Q; yield on loans fell 23bps but CoF fell faster (CLSA 4QFY26 note)
- CLSA confirmed this as a positive surprise: "NIM positive surprise — yield on loans fell 23bps (repo rate impact) but cost of funds fell faster"

Management's new framework: "ROA is what we should focus on" (CFO, Q4FY26 call) — this de-emphasises the NIM line and focuses markets on the returns outcome, which has been consistently delivered.

## 1.4 GOVERNANCE OVERHANG — HONEST ASSESSMENT

The resignation of Chairman Atanu Chakraborty on March 18, 2026 is the single most important near-term stock catalyst (negative). Key facts:

- Board was not informed of reasons; Macquarie interprets as potential power struggle with CEO Sashi Jagdishan
- New interim part-time Chairman Keki Mistry confirmed: no material matters, no operational issues
- RBI, GoI, SEBI issued supportive statements post-event
- Deposit flows remained stable and strong through the event — the Rs2.45tn 4QFY26 deposit accretion was AFTER the event (March 18 resignation, quarter-end March 31)
- CEO Sashi Jagdishan's contract renewal is due **October 2026** — the single biggest unresolved uncertainty
- Dubai branch matter: legal review in process; NCDRC ruling confirmed complainants were sophisticated investors pursuing high-yield products

**Alpha assessment:** The governance event has created an asymmetric risk/reward. If Jagdishan is reappointed (base case given track record, regulator support, and absence of operational failings), the governance discount should compress. If he is not reappointed, the market will need to price management risk premium until a successor is credibly established.

## 1.7 BEAR THESIS IN BRIEF (for Delta to build upon)

1. CEO departure risk is non-zero and unpriced
2. FY26 loan growth was 200bps below system — if this persists in FY27, ROE recovery thesis fails
3. NIM: With borrowings at 11% (vs 21%), the "margin improvement from borrowing rundown" argument has largely played out; further upside is limited
4. Consensus FY27E ROE of 13.97% is structurally below pre-merger levels (16-17%) and declining
5. CASA ratio erosion (48% in FY22 → 34% in FY26) reflects secular shift to time deposits — structurally higher CoF
6. Dubai branch matter: legal review incomplete; tail risk unquantified
