## 3.1 BEAR PILLAR 1: GOVERNANCE RISK IS UNRESOLVED AND BINARY

### The Resignation Event — What the Market Does Not Know

- Chairman Atanu Chakraborty resigned March 18, 2026 with **no reason given to the board**
- The board repeatedly asked him why; he gave no specific reason (Macquarie, March 19, 2026)
- Macquarie explicitly states: *"The new Chairman Keki Mistry also said that there could be some relationship issue between Atanu (ex-chairman) and the management which we interpret as a possible power struggle"*
- This is significant: Keki Mistry himself — the new Chairman — raised the possibility of a relationship issue

### The Jagdishan Succession Uncertainty

- CEO Sashidhar Jagdishan's contract renewal is due **October 2026** — 4 months from now
- This is the most material unresolved uncertainty in the bank's governance structure
- There is no named internal or external successor; no transition plan has been communicated
- If Jagdishan departs (voluntarily or otherwise), the bank loses:
  - The architect of the post-merger integration strategy
  - The face of the "responsible growth" and ROA-focused pivot
  - The primary relationship with regulators who have shown confidence in the current leadership team
- Delta assessment: There is a non-trivial probability (Delta estimates 20-25%) that the October 2026 renewal announcement either does not happen or is structured differently (e.g., transition timeline announced), creating a second governance overhang event

### Dubai Branch Matter — Unquantified Tail Risk

- CEO Q4FY26 call: *"There is also an NCDRC order, which came out on the 23rd of March, which highlights that the complainants are not retail in nature or are not uninformed investors"*
- Legal review is "in process" — timeline unspecified
- If the legal review surfaces additional regulatory action, the impact on the bank's regulatory capital or licence conditions is unquantifiable at this stage
- The framing suggests the bank is confident this will resolve benignly — but confidence is not resolution

**Bear Case Severity:** HIGH. The governance overhang cannot be resolved by balance sheet metrics. It is a multiple-compression factor independent of fundamentals. Macquarie removed HDFC Bank from the Marquee Buy list specifically for this reason.

## 3.2 BEAR PILLAR 2: LOAN GROWTH MISS IS A PATTERN, NOT AN ANOMALY

### FY26 Loan Growth Scorecard

- Management guidance at start of FY26: "grow in line with system" (system expected at ~11.5%)
- FY26 actual system growth (RBI data): ~13.5-14%
- HDFC Bank FY26A actual: **12.0%** [S&P Capital IQ]
- Shortfall vs system: **-150 to -200bps**

This is the direct Macquarie "Shifting Goalposts" critique: *"HDFC Bank missed FY26 loan growth guidance. The guidance for FY26 was to grow in line with the system, but HDFC Bank's 12% loan growth has been 200bps below system loan growth numbers."*

### The "Responsible Growth" Rationalisation — Scrutinised

Management's explanation on the Q4FY26 call (Sashi Jagdishan): *"We calibrated our strategies and our growth in line with [expected system credit growth of 10.5-11.5%]. The system... it comes to somewhere around 13.5% to 13.9%. That's the system growth. Obviously, it has been faster. It is something that we have to navigate."*

**Delta's critique:**
- The bank has now missed its system-pace guidance for 2 consecutive years: FY25 loan growth was 5.4% when the system grew ~14-15% (post-merger LDR constraint was the explanation)
- FY26 was supposed to be the year of catch-up; instead, the bank still grew below system
- The "responsible growth" narrative is legitimate in the context of post-merger LDR management, but this rationale is weakening as LDR normalises
- For FY27, management has now **dropped explicit growth guidance entirely** — *"Guidance on loan growth above system levels for FY27 is now replaced with sustaining loan growth momentum"* (Macquarie)
- This "shifting goalposts" pattern — guidance given, missed, then revised downward — is precisely the behaviour that triggers persistent P/BV discounts for banks

### Retail Loan Growth — The Structural Concern

- Retail loan growth FY26A: 6.5% YoY (Citi) — significantly below system retail credit growth
- Q4FY26 retail loan growth: 2.6% QoQ — underwhelming
- Corporate and SME loans drove the FY26 recovery — not retail
- HDFC Bank's franchise strength is in **retail** (salary accounts, mortgages, auto, credit cards)
- If the retail engine is not firing, the bank is growing in segments with thinner margins and less cross-sell value
- Retail to wholesale loan mix: ~53-54% retail (FY26A) vs historically 55-60%+ pre-merger

**Implication:** Corporate loan-heavy growth at below-market NIM yields and thinner fee income per relationship does not support the premium P/BV the bull case requires.

## 3.3 BEAR PILLAR 3: NIM RECOVERY IS LIMITED AND OVER-PROMISED

### The Borrowing Rundown Argument Is Largely Exhausted

The primary NIM bull argument has been: "As HDFC Ltd's high-cost borrowings run off, NIM improves." The data shows this is largely behind us:

- FY23A borrowings: Rs2.07tn (8.4% of BS)
- FY24A peak: Rs6.62tn (18.3% of BS) — HDFC Ltd absorbed
- FY26A: Rs4.89tn (11.2% of BS) — Rs1.7tn run down already
- FY28E: Rs4.30tn — only Rs0.6tn more to run down

Macquarie explicitly states: *"Borrowings declined to 11% of the balance sheet in FY26, down from 21% in FY23... confidence in a meaningful improvement in margins from the FY26 level of 3.35% appears limited."*

### The CASA Erosion Is Structural, Not Cyclical

| Year | CASA Ratio |
|------|-----------|
| FY22A | 48.2% |
| FY24A | 38.2% |
| FY26A | 34.2% |
| FY27E | 33.8% |
| FY28E | 33.7% |

CASA ratio is declining relentlessly. This is structural: Indian households are rationally moving savings into term deposits for higher yield returns; HDFC Bank's own retail deposit mix skews toward time deposits (Rs20.4tn time deposits vs Rs10.6tn CASA in FY26A). Consensus projects further CASA erosion to 33.7% by FY28E. At a structural CASA ratio of ~34% (vs 48% three years ago), the "low-cost funding advantage" that supported NIM of 4.0%+ in FY22-FY23 is permanently impaired.

### Rate Cycle Risk — The Hidden NIM Bear

- RBI has paused cuts at 6.0% (25bps in Feb + June + possibly October 2025 = total 125bps from 6.5%/6.25%)
- India-Pakistan geopolitical tensions (referenced in Q4FY26 earnings call) have caused market rates to harden
- If RBI does not cut further (or reverses direction), the deposit repricing benefit that consensus models as the FY27-28 NIM recovery driver simply does not materialise
- CFO on the Q4FY26 call: *"We don't want to hazard a guess whether the rate reduction cycle is done and it's bottomed and now it's going to start going up... the securities market... it seems to be going up"*
- This is management warning that NIM recovery is rate-path dependent — and the rate path has become uncertain

## 3.4 BEAR PILLAR 4: ROE TRAJECTORY — STRUCTURAL IMPAIRMENT?

### The Pre-Merger vs Post-Merger ROE Gap

| Period | ROE | Comment |
|--------|-----|---------|
| FY22A | 16.6% | Pre-merger standalone |
| FY23A | 17.0% | Pre-merger standalone |
| FY24A | 16.9% | First full year post-merger (distorted by timing) |
| FY25A | 14.3% | Full merger effect felt |
| FY26A | 14.0% | Continued drag |
| FY27E | 14.0% | NO IMPROVEMENT |
| FY28E | 14.6% | Marginal recovery |

The consensus ROE path recovers only to ~14.6% by FY28E — still 230bps below pre-merger levels. This is the Macquarie thesis on why despite the bank being "cheap," there is no near-term re-rating catalyst: *"Why do we like the stock? Cheap valuation and earnings growth... but the stock trades at 1.4x FY28E P/BV, 10x FY28E P/E."*

### The ROE Recovery Requires Multiple Things Going Right Simultaneously

For ROE to recover to 16%:
1. NIM must expand from 3.29% to ~3.60%+ (requires CASA stabilisation + rate cuts)
2. Loan growth must accelerate to 15%+ (requires retail reacceleration)
3. Credit costs must decline and stay at 0.40-0.45%
4. Opex ratio must fall further below 38%

Each of these individually has a base case path. But the bear case says: not all four will happen simultaneously in a 24-month window.

## 3.5 BEAR PILLAR 5: COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE — MARKET SHARE UNDER PRESSURE

### Peer Comparison Threat

- **ICICI Bank** has been gaining market share in retail lending, digital products, and credit cards for 3 consecutive years
- ICICI Bank's ROA (FY26A: ~2.3-2.4% — market estimate) significantly exceeds HDFC Bank's 1.80%
- **Kotak Mahindra Bank** maintains CASA advantage and has lower post-merger complexity
- **Axis Bank** has been aggressively rebuilding retail franchise post-Citi acquisition

HDFC Bank's market leadership in cash management, trade finance, and cards is well-established, but the ROA gap with ICICI Bank has **widened** post-merger. The premium P/BV that HDFC Bank historically commanded over ICICI Bank (typically 1.5-2.0x P/BV premium) has narrowed significantly.

### LDR Normalisation — The Competitive Catch-Up Risk

For 2 years, HDFC Bank's slower loan growth allowed peers to gain market share. Now that LDR has normalised (95%), the bank needs to demonstrate it can win back market share in segments where it was absent. There is no guarantee that the pipeline of corporate relationships can be rapidly switched to retail, or that borrowers who moved to competitors during HDFC Bank's growth slowdown will return.

## 3.6 BEAR PILLAR 6: CREDIT COST — PROVISIONING BUFFER CONCERN

### The FY26A Consensus Credit Cost Anomaly

- S&P Capital IQ FY26A credit cost: 0.83% — materially above management guidance of 0.45-0.50%
- This arises from FY26 provision volatility: large 1H charges (including the Rs90bn floating provision release in 1QFY26 that actually reduced provisions, and subsequent 2H rebuilding)
- If the 0.83% run-rate is the true normalised credit cost (rather than 0.35-0.45%), the profitability case is challenged
- Standard chartered and Citi models use ~0.45-0.55% for FY27E — aligning with management. But if consumer stress emerges (India-Pakistan tensions → economic slowdown → SME stress), credit costs could surprise

### HDB Financial Services — Hidden Risk

- HDFC Bank holds 74.12% of HDB Financial Services, an NBFC with Rs1.185tn loan book
- HDB's Gross Stage 3 assets: 2.44% — materially higher than the parent bank's 1.15% GNPA
- NBFC sector has faced regulatory tightening from RBI (higher risk weights on unsecured consumer credit)
- If HDB requires capital support or faces credit deterioration, the bank's effective NPA ratio on a consolidated basis rises
