## 2. FRANCHISE AND MACRO CONTEXT

HDFC Bank is India's largest private sector bank by assets (Rs43.6tn, FY26A), with a 30-year track record and market leadership across 8+ banking segments. The bank serves 100 million customers through 9,700 branches and a 60mn+ mobile banking user base.

The July 2023 merger with HDFC Limited was transformational in scale but disruptive in its financial impact:
- Balance sheet nearly doubled (HDFC Ltd Rs8.7tn mortgage book absorbed)
- Legacy borrowings of Rs6.6tn at above-market rates entered the book
- LDR rose to 110% — constraining loan growth for 2 years
- NIM declined from 4.05% (FY23A) to 3.29% (FY26A) — a 76bps structural compression
- ROE fell from 16.9% (FY24A) to 14.0% (FY26A)

As of June 2026, the structural overhang is substantially resolved:
- LDR: 110% (Dec 2023) → 95% (Mar 2026) [NON-CAPIQ: Macquarie Apr 2026]
- Borrowings: Rs6.6tn (FY24 peak) → Rs4.9tn (FY26A) [S&P Capital IQ, FY26A]
- The RBI Governor explicitly stated LDR is not a regulatory constraint

India's macro backdrop is constructive: nominal GDP growth ~9-9.5%, system credit growth ~13.5-14%, RBI repo rate at 5.75% (125bps of cuts since Feb 2025). The primary macro risk — India-Pakistan geopolitical tensions — is a near-term headwind but management characterises it as a "couple of months" dampener on corporate demand, not a structural credit event.

## 3. THE THREE COMMITTEE THEMES — ADJUDICATED VERDICT

### 3.1 Governance: Chairman Resignation and CEO Renewal Risk

**Facts established across pipeline stages:**
- Chairman Atanu Chakraborty resigned March 18, 2026 without disclosing reasons to the board (Macquarie, March 19, 2026)
- The new interim Chairman Keki Mistry described the event as possibly a "relationship issue" — signalling a power dynamic concern rather than operational or regulatory failure
- RBI, Government of India, and SEBI all issued supportive statements — a simultaneous endorsement from all three regulators is exceptionally rare and highly significant
- RBI approved Keki Mistry's appointment "within short notice" — further evidence of regulatory confidence
- Deposit flows in the quarter of the resignation (4QFY26) were the strongest in the bank's history: Rs2.45tn accretion. Depositors did not flee.
- CEO Sashidhar Jagdishan's contract renewal is due October 2026. No successor has been named. The NRC must decide before the contract expires.
- Dubai branch matter: disclosed in Q4FY26 notes to accounts; auditors have signed off; legal review in process. NCDRC ruling confirmed complainants were sophisticated investors pursuing high-yield products.

**IC adjudication:**
The governance event is the most material single risk factor at the moment. However, it is a binary event with a known resolution date (October 2026 — approximately 4 months from the date of this memo). The market has already discounted significant governance risk: the stock is down 62% from peak and 11% since the resignation event alone. We assess the probability of a positive CEO renewal outcome at 65% (consistent with Echo's CAT-1 analysis). The expected value of the renewal binary event is +7.15% (0.65 × +15% + 0.20 × −13% + 0.15 × 0%). There is no evidence of any regulatory, financial, or operational failure that would justify non-renewal — the 4QFY26 results demonstrate this. The governance risk is real but it is a discount opportunity, not a franchise impairment event.

**Conclusion:** GOVERNANCE RISK IS REAL, TIMEBOUND, AND PRICED. It does not override the fundamental case.

### 3.2 NIM Trajectory: Borrowing Rundown vs Residual Tailwinds

**The debate:**
- **Bear (Macquarie "Shifting Goalposts" position):** "Borrowings declined to 11% of the balance sheet in FY26, down from 21% in FY23. Confidence in a meaningful improvement in margins from the FY26 level of 3.35% appears limited." The major arbitrage from replacing high-cost HDFC Ltd borrowings with deposits has been captured. Remaining borrowings Rs4.9tn (FY26A) declining to Rs4.3tn (FY28E) — only Rs0.6tn more to run off over 2 years.
- **Bull (CLSA 4QFY26; Citi 3QFY26):** The NIM surprise in 4QFY26 came from deposit cost falling faster than loan yield. Cost-of-funds repricing still has 5-6 quarters to run on existing time deposit maturities. India rate cycle (if resumed) provides further tailwind. Management explicitly de-emphasised NIM and re-focused on ROA, which has stabilised at 1.80-1.96%.

**IC adjudication:**
Both positions are correct in their own framing. Macquarie is right that the borrowing-rundown tailwind is mostly exhausted — from Rs6.6tn to Rs4.3tn over 3 years is the correct picture. However, the bear case over-weights this single mechanism while under-weighting the deposit repricing channel. CFO Vaidyanathan confirmed on the Q4FY26 call that only 40-50bps of the 125bps RBI cuts have passed through to term deposit costs so far. The remaining 75-85bps of deposit repricing over the next 4-6 quarters is a distinct and separate NIM tailwind from borrowings. Consensus NIM recovery from 3.29% (FY26A) to 3.45% (FY28E) of +16bps is achievable without any new rate cuts simply from time deposit rollover at lower rates.

The NIM debate is ultimately not decisive for the investment case, because management's own framework ("ROA over NIM") has been validated: ROA is stable at ~1.80-1.96%, and the consensus path recovers ROA toward 1.95% by FY30E without requiring NIM to return to 4.05%.

**Conclusion:** NIM RECOVERY IS PARTIAL AND DEPOSIT-DRIVEN, NOT BORROWING-DRIVEN. 16bps of improvement (FY26A → FY28E) is achievable and is the right base case. The Macquarie bear on NIM is directionally correct but overstated as a downside scenario.

### 3.3 Loan Growth Miss: Pattern or Catch-Up?

**The facts:**
- FY25A loan growth: 5.4% — defended as post-merger LDR constraint management
- FY26A loan growth: 12% vs system 13.5-14% — a 200bps miss vs system
- Retail loan growth FY26A: 6.5% YoY (Citi) — materially below system retail credit growth
- Corporate and SME loans drove the recovery — not retail
- Management dropped explicit "grow above system" guidance for FY27, replaced with "sustaining momentum"

**IC adjudication:**
The loan growth miss is the most objectively verifiable piece of negative evidence in this case. Management's FY26 guidance was to grow "in line with system." System grew 13.5%. The bank grew 12%. This is a factual miss. The "responsible growth" rationalisation is partially credible — the LDR was a genuine constraint for most of FY25-FY26 — but Macquarie's critique is valid: the system consistently outgrew expectations, and the bank had an opportunity to do the same. The decision not to was conscious, but the outcome was a market share loss.

However, the forward-looking case is more positive than the backward-looking miss suggests:
1. LDR at 95% — the constraint is genuinely removed (not a rationalisation)
2. 1QFY27 will be the first quarter where management can demonstrate growth re-acceleration without the LDR excuse
3. Retail disbursement momentum is improving: mortgages at 8,000+ origination locations, auto loans growing, digital channels activated
4. Business banking (MSME) grew 20% YoY in FY26 — a high-quality segment

The 1QFY27 results (July 2026) are the critical data point. If loan growth prints 14-15% with retail participation, the "pattern" narrative breaks. If it prints sub-12%, the Macquarie thesis is confirmed and the stock will likely re-test its 52-week low.

**Conclusion:** LOAN GROWTH MISS IS REAL, BUT FORWARD POSITIONING IS BETTER THAN THE STOCK PRICE IMPLIES. The July 2026 earnings are the single highest-priority monitoring event before the CEO renewal.

## 11. MOAT AND QUALITY ASSESSMENT

HDFC Bank's franchise moat was assessed at **4.2/5 — Wide Moat, temporarily impaired** (Stage 6 Golf). The moat has not been structurally damaged by the merger or the governance event. Key evidence:
- 30-year GNPA below 1.5% through 3 business cycles — the credit culture is institutional, not person-dependent
- 26-28% credit card spend share — this is a network effect, not a manager effect
- 200-300bps deposit market share gains in FY26 — even in the governance event quarter
- The mortgage cross-sell (50% liability penetration, up from 36%) is an operationally embedded process, not a CEO-specific strategy

The key moat risk is: if Jagdishan departs and his successor does not maintain the "ROA discipline over growth at any cost" culture. This is a genuine concern but it is (a) low probability and (b) identifiable ex-post (observable in the next 2-4 quarterly results).
