### Why the DCF looks "low" ($135)
Don't be fooled by the low DCF number — it's a math artifact, not a verdict.
NVDA's beta of 2.20 forces a punitive 16.56% discount rate (WACC). When you use a high
discount rate on near-term cash flows that are exploding, the model undervalues them. The
*market* effectively prices NVDA with a much lower discount rate (implied ~8.7%) because
it's treating NVIDIA like a durable franchise, not a volatile semiconductor stock. The
forward P/E of 16.1× on 80%-growing earnings is the more honest valuation lens here.

### NVDA: What's cheap vs what's expensive

| Multiple | NVDA | Peer Median | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forward P/E | **16.1×** | 21.7× | NVDA is **26% CHEAPER** than peers |
| EV/EBITDA | 29.8× | 44.3× | NVDA cheaper |
| EV/Revenue | 19.4× | 19.0× | Roughly in line |
| Revenue growth | +85% | +35% | NVDA growing **2.4× faster** |
| Gross margin | 74% | 55% | NVDA far superior |

**The core bull argument:** NVDA is the *cheapest* large-cap semiconductor on forward
earnings while simultaneously having the *highest* growth, *highest* margins, and *best*
FCF generation. A P/E discount to slower-growing peers makes no logical sense unless you
believe growth is about to fall off a cliff.
