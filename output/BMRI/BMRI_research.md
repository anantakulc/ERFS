## Section 2 — Company Overview & Business Model

**What Bank Mandiri Does** — PT Bank Mandiri (Persero) Tbk, listed on the Indonesia Stock Exchange (IDX: BMRI), is Indonesia's largest bank by assets (~Rp 2,470tn, FY25). Formed in 1998 from the merger of four state banks during the Asian financial crisis and IPO'd in 2003, it is the flagship state-owned (Persero) universal bank, regulated by OJK and operating under Bank Indonesia monetary policy. Its franchise is wholesale-led — corporate and commercial banking for SOEs and large Indonesian corporates, with a deep transaction-banking and cash-management moat — complemented by a growing retail and SME business (mortgages, payroll, auto finance via Mandiri Tunas Finance, micro/KUR). Its digital platforms — Livin' (retail super-app, 25mn+ users) and Kopra (wholesale) — are the best of the state-bank cluster. As of February 2025–2026, state ownership has been reorganised under Danantara (sovereign wealth fund) via the PTBKI operational holding. Fiscal year ends 31 December.

**Business Model Flywheel**
```
Wholesale/SOE ecosystem (corporate + transaction banking)
        │
        ▼
CASA + payroll deposits (~68% CASA) ──► competitive cost of funds
        │                                        │
        ▼                                        ▼
Loan growth (wholesale + digital retail)   NIM (~4.6–4.7%)
        │                                        │
        ▼                                        ▼
Livin'/Kopra fees + low credit cost (NPL 0.98%) ──► PPOP
        │
        ▼
ROE ~21% ──► high dividend (~78% payout, ~7.7% yield) + capital retention
        │
        ▼
Balance-sheet growth (largest in Indonesia) ──► repeat
        │
        ▼
[Governance leakage: Danantara extraction mandate diverts part of the loop]
```

**Key franchise metrics**
- Largest Indonesian bank by assets (~Rp 2,470tn FY25); system-leading wholesale franchise.
- ROE ~21% (ttm) — near BBCA, far above BBNI (~11–14%).
- Gross NPL 0.98% (1Q26) — cleanest of the big-four.
- Livin' 25mn+ users; Kopra anchors SOE/corporate transaction flows.

## Section 12 — Bull Case (10 Catalysts)

**Thesis:** BMRI is the cheapest ROE-adjusted big-four bank — ~1.2x P/BV on ~21% ROE — with the cleanest credit book in its history and a ~7.7% dividend yield, while raw Gordon-Growth logic justifies ~1.8x. If the Danantara governance overhang clarifies favourably and NIM holds, the stock can re-rate toward Rp 6,000+ while paying you to wait.

1. **FY26 dividend confirmation (~Rp 500 DPS, ~7.7% yield).** Sustained ~78% payout re-anchors the income thesis. At Rp 4,060 the carry alone delivers a high single-digit return floor while the re-rating develops; a confirmed or raised payout would crystallise the value case and is the highest-probability near-term positive.
2. **Cleanest asset quality of the big-four.** Gross NPL 0.98%, net 0.41% (1Q26) with declining cost of credit. This is the strongest BMRI differentiator vs BBRI (contractor/micro stress) and supports a quality re-rating; provisioning conservatism leaves room for write-backs.
3. **Deep ROE-adjusted value vs raw GGM.** A 20% ROE franchise at 1.2x P/BV against a raw GGM justified 1.81x is a ~37% theoretical gap. Any narrowing of the governance discount — even halfway — implies Rp 5,000–5,500.
4. **NIM stabilisation on asset-yield repricing.** Higher IDR government-bond yields lift asset yields; if funding costs plateau, NIM can hold at 4.7% vs the cut 4.5–4.7% guide, beating expectations and supporting NII.
5. **Livin'/Kopra digital monetisation.** 25mn+ Livin' users and Kopra wholesale flows drive CASA, fees, and operating leverage — the most durable, internally-controlled moat among state banks; continued user/fee growth is a structural earnings tailwind.
6. **BI rate-cut pivot.** If the IDR stabilises sub-Rp 16,500, BI can resume easing, directly compressing CoE from ~13% toward ~12% and mechanically lifting justified P/BV by ~0.2x — a powerful re-rating lever.
7. **Danantara dividend-friendly outcome.** If the sovereign fund formalises BMRI as a high-payout cash generator (consistent with the ≥5%/$50bn government-transfer mandate via dividends rather than equity dilution), the yield thesis strengthens and the governance discount narrows.
8. **MSCI EM status retained.** Removal of the EM→Frontier reclassification overhang triggers a relief rally across large-cap Indonesian banks; BMRI's significant index weight makes it a direct beneficiary.
9. **Wholesale pipeline delivery in 2H26.** FDI, downstreaming (nickel), data-centre and infrastructure lending converting to drawn loans validates the organic 7–9% target and reduces reliance on BRIS base effects.
10. **Mean-reversion to historical P/BV.** BMRI's 5-year average P/BV (~1.8x) sits far above the current 1.2x; a normalisation of the Indonesian macro narrative alone (without fundamental change) implies substantial upside.

## Section 13 — Bear Case (10 Thesis-Breakers)

**Thesis:** The headline strength is an accounting illusion, the governance regime has structurally changed against minorities, and the macro/CoE backdrop caps the re-rating — leaving a fairly-valued high-yield SOE, not a cheap compounder. Downside to Rp ~3,300 (−19%).

1. **BRIS-deconsolidation illusion.** 1Q26's +16.2% loan growth and +16.6% profit are flattered by the 1 Jan 2026 deconsolidation of Bank Syariah Indonesia. Organic loan guidance is only 7–9% (half of FY25's 13.4%), and the loss of the high-margin sharia book is precisely why FY26 NIM guidance was *cut*. Bulls extrapolating the headline are mispricing a base effect.
2. **Danantara minority-shareholder regime change.** State ownership moved to PTBKI, and Danantara AM bought BMRI's asset-management subsidiary (1 Apr 2026) — franchise value leaving the listed entity. With a government cash-extraction mandate (≥5%/$50bn/yr) and a directive to redeploy SOE dividends into political-priority sectors, capital allocation is no longer ROE-governed. This is the structural reason BMRI deserves a discount to BBCA.
3. **Structural NIM compression with the thinnest buffer.** FY26 NIM cut to 4.5–4.7% (from ~5.0%). CASA only ~68% (vs BBCA 85%) and a wholesale, rate-sensitive funding base means BMRI repays the BI 5.25% environment faster on liabilities. FY25 already showed interest expense +17.6% vs income +8.7%. Downside to ~4.4% NIM costs several trillion Rupiah of NII.
4. **SOE-contractor / directed-lending credit tail.** Gross NPL 0.98% is a cycle low — it can only worsen. BMRI carries the system's elevated SOE-contractor exposure (~Rp 121tn impaired system-wide) plus commodity-cyclical corporates. A cycle turn plus mandated below-market lending (KUR, cooperatives) could lift cost of credit 30–50bps and pull ROE toward 18%.
5. **CoE kills the re-rating.** Post-BI-hike, with 10yr IDR ~7%, beta ~1.05 and an SOE risk premium, CoE is ~13–13.5%. At ROE 19% and a governance haircut, justified P/BV falls to ~1.0–1.1x — near the current price. The stock is fairly valued, not cheap.
6. **Management credibility on margin.** 3 of 6 recent commitments missed/cut (FY25 NIM miss, flat FY25 profit, FY26 NIM guide-down — Delta §3.3). The pattern is over-promise on margin, deliver on volume — a reason to discount forward NIM guidance.
7. **IDR and sovereign stress.** IDR near record lows, Fitch Negative outlook (Mar 2026), persistent foreign outflows. For USD investors, each leg of IDR depreciation erodes returns regardless of local earnings; the macro is a regime, not a dip.
8. **MSCI EM→Frontier reclassification.** A binary, systemic forced-selling event; BMRI's large index weight makes it a direct casualty of any downgrade, independent of fundamentals.
9. **Populist directed-lending escalation.** Under Prabowo, SOE banks are the primary policy-delivery vehicle. New subsidised/cooperative mandates at capped yields would directly compress BMRI's ROE more than any private peer.
10. **Dividend permanence risk.** The DDM (Rp 5,825) assumes a stable ~78% payout in perpetuity. If Danantara redirects capital to equity injections for priority projects instead of dividends, the entire income thesis — the core of the return — breaks.
